Part 01

The Six Principles

Every image at ACKO — photographed, illustrated, or AI-generated — must pass all six. These are the why behind every decision that follows.

🤝
01
Warm, not clinical
Soft natural light. Familiar, lived-in moments. No sterile boardrooms, stiff stock smiles, or cold corporate polish.
☀️
02
Bright and modern
Light-filled contemporary scenes. Nothing dated or heavy. Should look like it was made this year, not five years ago.
🎯
03
Disciplined, not decorative
Composed and considered. Clean frames, room around the subject. Warmth lives in the moment captured, not decoration.
😄
04
Playful in spirit, not execution
A small laugh, an easy gesture — but the craft stays grown-up. No quirky props, no visual gags, no exaggerated angles.
👤
05
Real, not cartoon
Real people, real objects, real environments. No mascots, no animated characters. ACKO is human, not a character.
⚡
06
Not traditional insurance
Within five seconds, a stranger sees a modern brand — not a legacy insurer or a PSU ad. If it could be anyone's, it's wrong.

Part 02 · People

Who We Show & How

Diversity is the default, not the exception. Cast deliberately across five axes — every campaign should cover at least three.

✓ Do
  • Show real-looking Indian people across skin tones, ages, regions, body types, and abilities
  • Cast across ≥3 axes per campaign: skin tone, age, region, body type, life stage
  • Capture mid-action moments — buckling a seatbelt, scrolling a phone, packing a bag
  • Use gentle expressions — small smiles, calm eyes, natural body language
  • Dress people in contemporary, regionally honest Indian clothing
  • Show older adults, people with disabilities, and larger body types as regular subjects
✗ Don't
  • Use celebrities, public figures, or recognisable lookalikes
  • Show forced 'happy family' poses lined up at the camera
  • Default to light-skinned, urban, 25-year-old models
  • Show plastic AI skin, melted hands, or distorted eyes
  • Show real ACKO employees, customers, or partners without written consent
  • Dress people in clothing or contexts they wouldn't actually wear
🎨
Skin Tone
Full Indian spectrum, weighted realistically. Don't default to fair.
🗺️
Region
North, South, East, West, Northeast — visible in clothing and setting.
🎂

Millennials, Gen X, older adults — not just 25-year-olds.
🏃

🏠

Single, parent, caregiver, retiree — each opens different moments.

Part 02 · Place

Where Things Happen

Settings feel lived-in and current. Indian cues — number plates, signage, architecture — belong wherever they'd naturally appear.

✓ Do
  • Use real Indian settings — homes, streets, hospitals, vehicles, offices
  • Show right-hand drive cars and Indian number plates
  • Include Indian signage and street details where natural
  • Keep settings lived-in and current, not aspirational or staged
  • Match the setting to the moment: calm for care, active for daily life
✗ Don't
  • Use foreign settings passed off as Indian (left-hand drive, foreign hospitals)
  • Use empty studio backgrounds when a real setting tells the story better
  • Show outdated environments — old PSU offices, dated interiors, dated tech
  • Include real brand names, hospital names, or competitor visuals

Part 02 · Look & Feel

Colour, Light & Composition

Warmth lives in the moment we capture — not in filters or decoration around it.

✓ Do
  • Soft, natural daylight as the default — golden hour and warm indoor light both work
  • Natural, honest skin tones — amber and peach grading, slightly desaturated
  • One clear subject — room to breathe around it, clean background
  • Shallow depth of field (f/2.8 range) — sharp subject, softly blurred background
  • Test composition across 1:1, 9:16, 16:9, and 4:5 before finalising
  • Gentle film grain for warmth and authenticity
✗ Don't
  • Use neon colours, muddy darks, or clashing palettes
  • Over-saturate or apply heavy orange/yellow filters
  • Use harsh studio light, hard shadows, or glossy "AI render" finishes
  • Crop in ways that break faces, hands, or key context
  • Show fear, shock, panic, blood, distress, or accident gore
  • Show ecstatic, theatrical, or exaggerated emotions
Part 03 · Prompt Guidelines

Writing Prompts That Work

Every strong ACKO prompt has five building blocks. Miss any one of them and the image drifts — wrong tone, wrong setting, wrong energy. These come directly from prompts that have been approved and used.

1
Subject
Who is in frame. Be specific about ethnicity, age range, and clothing. Not "a woman" — name the context.
e.g. "A young Indian woman in her early thirties wearing a casual light blue shirt"
2
Action
What they're doing. Mid-motion beats posed. The action should feel like a moment you walked into.
e.g. "standing calmly beside his car, phone gently held to her ear, listening attentively"
3
Setting
Where it happens — Indian context, always specified. Mention specific Indian visual cues.
e.g. "clean, well-lit Indian car service workshop" or "quiet residential street"
4
Mood & Expression
The emotional register. ACKO's band: composed, calm, trusting, quietly reassured. Never distressed.
e.g. "composed and trusting, as if being guided through the next steps"
5
Light & Grade
Lighting style, colour grade, and technical feel. This line is nearly identical across all approved prompts.
e.g. "soft diffused natural light, warm golden hour tones, amber and peach grading, gentle film grain"
10 Rules Extracted From Real Prompts

These patterns appear consistently across every approved ACKO prompt. They're not arbitrary — they're what makes the images feel like ACKO.

📐
Always specify 16:9 widescreen banner
All approved prompts use "16:9 widescreen banner" as the format. This is the default output format for banners and campaigns.
📸
50mm lens at f/2.8 — every time
This technical spec produces the shallow depth of field and cinematic feel ACKO images are known for. Never specify wide-angle or fish-eye.
🎭
Always say "Candid documentary feel, not posed"
This phrase is in every approved prompt. It prevents the AI from defaulting to stock-photo stiffness and keeps moments natural.
🌅
The standard grading line — copy it exactly
"Soft diffused natural light, warm golden hour tones, amber and peach color grading, slightly desaturated, gentle film grain." Use this verbatim.
💆
Always add "no heavy makeup"
Without this, AI tools default to over-styled faces. This single phrase keeps skin natural and relatable.
🏠
End with the aesthetic anchor
"Middle-class Indian aesthetic, modern but understated." This is the brand's tone in three words — always close with it.
😌
Describe calm, not distress
Even in stressful moments (accident, stolen car, lost passport), the subject is composed and engaged — never panicked. ACKO is the reassurance, not the drama.
🚗
Add Indian vehicle cues explicitly
When a car is in frame, specify "compact silver sedan with an Indian number plate." AI tools default to Western cars. Always name it.
🌫️
Always blur the background
"Background softly blurred" appears in every prompt. It keeps the subject clear and prevents the setting from competing for attention.
🎬
Open with the shot format
Start every prompt with "Cinematic photorealistic lifestyle photograph, 16:9 widescreen banner, shot on 50mm lens at f/2.8, shallow depth of field." This primes the model correctly.
Master Template
Copy, fill the slots, ship it

Subject slot
Always specify: gender, approximate age, what they're wearing, and one behavioural cue. "A young Indian man in his early thirties wearing a casual light blue shirt and dark jeans."
Setting slot
Indian context, always. Name it specifically: "clean, well-lit Indian car service workshop", "quiet residential street", "modern Indian clinic". Avoid generic or abstract settings.
The grading line

Part 04 · AI Generation

AI Image Rules

Most ACKO imagery is AI-generated. These rules keep output safe, consistent, and legally clean.

✓ Do
  • Use approved tools only, on commercial-use plans
  • Follow the five-part prompt framework — Subject, Action, Setting, Mood, Light
  • Inspect every image: hands, eyes, text on signs, repeating patterns
  • Save the prompt, tool name, and seed alongside the final asset
  • Label images 'Illustrative' if they could be mistaken for a real event
  • Human review before publishing — always
✗ Don't
  • Reference copyrighted styles ("in the style of Pixar", "like Studio Ghibli")
  • Name competitors, athletes, or public figures in prompts
  • Use unlicensed reference photos in image-to-image tools
  • Publish AI images with visible errors — even small ones
  • Auto-publish without a human review step

Common Failures & Fixes

FailureFix
Plastic, perfect skinAdd "natural skin texture, slight imperfections, candid" to the prompt.
Wrong or melted handsGenerate at higher resolution; crop hands out; or regenerate with explicit hand placement.
Off or uncanny eyesRegenerate or use a touch-up tool. Never publish as-is — even subtle wrongness reads.
Generic Western settingAdd Indian cues: vehicle type, signage language, clothing, architecture.
Stock-photo smileSpecify "candid, mid-conversation, unposed".
Garbled signage or textRemove in post, or specify "no visible text" in the prompt.
Identical faces across a campaignVary prompts deliberately on age, region, and body type.
Part 05 · Compliance

IRDAI & Legal Requirements

Non-negotiable. No creative exception overrides these.

No false promises
Images cannot promise what the policy doesn't deliver.
Disclaimer visible
The standard IRDAI disclaimer must appear alongside any benefit claim.
Written consent
No real customers, employees, or public figures without written consent.
Legal review
Anything tied to a product claim goes through legal review before publishing.
Approval records
Keep records of prompts, tools, and approvals for every image.
Scope check
Don't update images across markets without checking the original approval scope.
